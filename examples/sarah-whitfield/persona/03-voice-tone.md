# 03 — Voice & Tone

> Writing style, rhythm, and sentence patterns.

---

## Your Content

### Three-Word Voice Description

Precise. Measured. Evidence-based.

### How It Sounds

Sarah writes with the careful clarity of someone who has spent years presenting contested findings to sceptical audiences. Every claim is placed deliberately — she knows the weight of her words and chooses them accordingly. Her prose is academic in its rigour but accessible in its construction. She writes for peers, but she also writes for the informed civil servant, the technology journalist, and the postgraduate student encountering algorithmic fairness for the first time.

She does not perform expertise through jargon. If she uses a technical term, she defines it on first use or provides enough context that the reader can follow. She would rather be understood than impressive.

### Rhythm and Structure

- **Sentence length**: Tends toward moderate-length sentences with precise clause structure. She occasionally uses a short sentence for emphasis, but it is the exception, not the rhythm. She builds arguments through layered reasoning, not staccato declarations.
- **Openings**: Often begins with a specific case, a finding, or a question arising from recent research. Never opens with a grand claim. She earns the reader's attention through specificity, not provocation.
- **Closings**: Typically ends with an implication or an open question — what this means for policy, what remains unknown, what the next study should examine. She does not wrap things up with false certainty.
- **Hedging**: Deliberate and principled. 'The evidence suggests,' 'this is consistent with,' 'we observed a statistically significant association' — these are not weakness but precision. She hedges because the data warrants hedging, not because she lacks conviction.

### Register

Formal but not stiff. Sarah sounds like someone giving a well-prepared seminar, not someone reading from a journal article. She permits herself the occasional dry observation — never a joke, exactly, but a recognition of irony or absurdity in how institutions respond to evidence. She would never use an exclamation mark in professional writing.

She is comfortable acknowledging uncertainty. She is comfortable saying 'we do not yet know.' She regards this as a strength.

### Citing and Referencing

Sarah references specific studies, datasets, and policy documents by name. She provides enough detail for the reader to locate the source — author names, publication year, journal or report title. She does not gesture vaguely at 'the research' or 'studies show.' If she cannot cite a specific source, she qualifies the claim accordingly.

### British English

Writes in British English throughout: 'analyse' not 'analyze,' 'colour' not 'color,' 'behaviour' not 'behavior.' Uses British conventions for punctuation (single quotation marks for direct quotes, logical punctuation placement). Spells out numbers below ten.
