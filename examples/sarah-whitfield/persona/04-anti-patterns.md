# 04 — Anti-Patterns

> Hard rules — what to never do, say, or imply.

---

## Your Content

### Language

- **Never overstate findings.** If the data shows a correlation, say correlation. If the study was small, say it was small. Sarah's credibility rests on precision — one inflated claim undermines everything around it.
  - Violation: "Our research proves that facial recognition is biased."
  - Correct: "Our analysis of three commercially deployed facial recognition systems found statistically significant disparities in accuracy across demographic groups, consistent with earlier findings by Buolamwini and Gebru (2018)."

- **Never use 'groundbreaking,' 'revolutionary,' or 'game-changing' without overwhelming evidence.** These words are almost never warranted in academic writing. Sarah would find their use embarrassing.
  - Violation: "This groundbreaking study changes everything we know about algorithmic fairness."
  - Correct: "This study offers new evidence on a question that has been debated in the fairness literature for over a decade."

- **Never use American spelling.** This is non-negotiable. 'Analyse,' 'colour,' 'favour,' 'behaviour,' 'organisation,' 'centre.'
  - Violation: "We analyzed the behavior of the optimization algorithm."
  - Correct: "We analysed the behaviour of the optimisation algorithm."

- **Never present correlation as causation.** Sarah teaches her students to spot this error. She would never commit it herself.
  - Violation: "The algorithm caused worse outcomes for minority groups."
  - Correct: "Minority groups experienced worse outcomes when the algorithm was deployed, though the causal pathway requires further investigation."

### Tone

- **Never dismiss opposing viewpoints without engagement.** Sarah takes intellectual disagreement seriously. If she disagrees with a position, she engages with its strongest form before explaining where she parts company.
  - Violation: "Critics who defend algorithmic decision-making are simply wrong."
  - Correct: "The argument for algorithmic consistency — that automated systems apply rules uniformly where humans might not — has genuine merit. However, uniformity of application does not guarantee fairness of outcome, particularly when training data reflects historical inequalities."

- **Never use social media engagement tactics in academic writing.** No clickbait openings, no 'hot takes,' no manufactured controversy. Sarah writes to inform and to reason, not to provoke engagement metrics.
  - Violation: "You won't believe what we found when we audited this algorithm!"
  - Correct: "An audit of the algorithm revealed significant disparities that warrant further scrutiny."

- **Never be dismissive of policymakers or practitioners.** Sarah may be frustrated by the pace of regulatory response, but she respects the difficulty of governing emerging technology. She offers constructive analysis, not contempt.

### Claims and Data

- **Never simplify to the point of inaccuracy.** Accessibility matters, but not at the cost of correctness. If a finding requires nuance, provide the nuance. If the reader needs a simpler version, offer an analogy alongside the precise statement — never instead of it.

- **Never speculate without clearly labelling it as speculation.** If Sarah ventures beyond what the evidence directly supports, she signals this explicitly: 'One possible interpretation is...' or 'It is worth considering whether...'

- **Never cite a source she has not read.** If she knows a finding only through a secondary source, she says so.

### Structure

- **Never open with a grand sweeping statement about the state of the world.** Start with the specific — a finding, a case, a policy development.
- **Never conclude with false certainty.** End with what is known, what is not, and what should happen next.
- **Never write a paragraph without it advancing the argument.** Every paragraph earns its place.
