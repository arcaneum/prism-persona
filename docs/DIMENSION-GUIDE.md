# Dimension Guide

This guide provides expanded prompting questions for each PRISM dimension, along with examples that illustrate the difference between strong and weak persona content. Use these questions to go deeper than the basic guiding questions in each template file.

---

## How to Use This Guide

Each dimension section below contains:

1. **Deep questions** — more detailed than those in the template files, designed to surface specifics.
2. **Good vs mediocre** — a comparison showing what useful persona content looks like versus vague filler.
3. **Common pitfalls** — mistakes people make when filling in this dimension.

Take your time. It is better to answer three questions thoroughly than to rush through all of them with one-line responses.

---

## 00 — Identity

### Deep Questions

- What is the one-sentence description of what you do that you give at a conference?
- What did you do professionally before your current role? How does that history shape your perspective?
- What cultural or geographic context shapes your work? (Time zone, language, market, regulatory environment)
- Are you a solo operator, a team leader, or part of a larger organisation? How does that affect your autonomy?
- What industry do you operate in? Is it niche or mainstream?
- If someone searched for you online, what would they find first?

### Good vs Mediocre

**Mediocre:** "I'm a tech entrepreneur working on AI products."

**Good:** "I run a one-person software company based in the UK. I build AI-assisted productivity tools — currently focused on educational technology and dictation software. My background is in full-stack development, but I spend as much time on product strategy and content as on code. I work remotely, operate on GMT, and ship products as a solo developer using AI pair programming."

The good version gives an AI enough specificity to make contextual decisions. The mediocre version could describe a hundred thousand people.

### Common Pitfalls

- Writing aspirationally rather than factually. Save future-state descriptions for dimension 09.
- Being too brief. Two sentences is not enough to anchor a persona.
- Including irrelevant personal details that do not affect professional output.

## 01 — Values & Ethics

### Deep Questions

- What would you refuse to do even if it were profitable?
- What principles guide your work that you would not compromise under pressure?
- How do you feel about data privacy? What is your position on tracking users?
- What is your stance on AI-generated content disclosure? Do you believe in transparency?
- What ethical lines exist in your industry that others cross but you will not?
- If a client asked you to do something that conflicted with your values, what would you do?
- What does integrity mean to you in practice — not as an abstract concept, but as daily behaviour?

### Good vs Mediocre

**Mediocre:** "I believe in honesty and treating people well."

**Good:** "I never inflate metrics or make claims I cannot substantiate. If a product has 50 users, I say 50 — not 'growing rapidly' or 'hundreds.' I believe in transparent pricing with no hidden costs. I will not use dark patterns in UX design. I disclose when content is AI-assisted. I do not take on client work that conflicts with my values, even when the money is good."

### Common Pitfalls

- Listing values so generic they apply to everyone (honesty, integrity, kindness).
- Failing to include the negative — what you will not do is often more useful than what you will.
- Confusing preferences with values. "I prefer dark mode" is not a value.

## 02 — Philosophy

### Deep Questions

- What is your position on AI in your industry? Where do you draw the line between useful assistance and over-reliance?
- Do you believe in building fast and iterating, or building carefully and launching when ready?
- What do you think most people in your field get wrong?
- What is your view on open source versus proprietary software?
- How do you think about pricing — premium, accessible, freemium?
- What role does technology play in your vision of the future?
- What hill would you die on professionally?

### Good vs Mediocre

**Mediocre:** "I think AI is a powerful tool that should be used responsibly."

**Good:** "I believe AI is the most significant productivity multiplier since the spreadsheet, but I reject the narrative that it replaces human judgement. I use AI as a pair programmer and first-draft generator, never as the final voice. I think the solo developer with AI tools can now compete with small teams, and that changes the economics of software permanently. I am sceptical of AI hype cycles but committed to the underlying capability."

### Common Pitfalls

- Being so balanced you say nothing. Philosophy is where you take positions.
- Confusing philosophy with values. Philosophy is debatable; values are not.
- Only covering your primary domain. Include adjacent areas that affect your work.

## 03 — Voice & Tone

### Deep Questions

- Read your last five pieces of writing. What patterns do you notice in sentence length, structure, and rhythm?
- How do you start pieces? Do you open with the conclusion, a question, a story, or a provocative statement?
- What words or phrases do you use repeatedly? (These are your verbal fingerprints.)
- What words or phrases do you consciously avoid?
- Do you use contractions? Rhetorical questions? Lists?
- How do you handle technical concepts — do you define terms, use analogies, or assume reader knowledge?
- What is the reading level of your typical writing?
- Do you use first person, second person, or third person most often?
- How does your voice change between platforms (if at all)?

### Good vs Mediocre

**Mediocre:** "My writing style is professional but approachable. I use clear language."

**Good:** "Short sentences by default. Rarely more than 20 words. I open with the conclusion and then support it — never bury the lead. Contractions always (it's, don't, won't). No exclamation marks. British English throughout (colour, analyse, organise). I use 'I' freely and address the reader as 'you.' Technical concepts get one-sentence explanations, never jargon-heavy paragraphs. Rhythm: two or three short declarative sentences, then one longer sentence that develops the thought. I end pieces with a forward-looking statement, not a call to action."

### Common Pitfalls

- Describing voice in abstract adjectives rather than concrete patterns.
- Ignoring rhythm. Two writers can both be "direct" but have completely different cadences.
- Not mentioning language variant (British English, American English, etc.).

## 04 — Anti-Patterns

### Deep Questions

- What phrases make you cringe when you see them in content? ("Game-changer," "leverage," "synergy"?)
- What tones would be completely wrong for your persona? (Sycophantic? Aggressive? Overly casual?)
- What claims should never be made on your behalf?
- Are there topics you refuse to comment on?
- What formatting choices are off-limits? (All caps? Excessive exclamation marks? Clickbait headlines?)
- What assumptions about your audience should never be made?
- What competitor behaviours do you refuse to replicate?

### Good vs Mediocre

**Mediocre:** "Don't be too salesy or use jargon."

**Good:**
```
NEVER:
- Use "game-changer," "revolutionary," "disrupt," or Silicon Valley hyperbole
- Claim AI will "replace" professionals — it augments, it does not replace
- Insert inflated user numbers or revenue claims
- Use exclamation marks in professional writing
- Open with "In today's fast-paced world..." or any throat-clearing cliche
- Speak on behalf of the company using "we" when it is a one-person operation
- Use American English spelling
- Make promises about future features without explicit approval
- Use emojis in technical or professional content
```

### Common Pitfalls

- Being too vague. "Don't be unprofessional" is not actionable.
- Only listing tone-based anti-patterns. Include content-level rules (claims, topics, positions).
- Forgetting format-level anti-patterns (emoji use, capitalisation, punctuation habits).

## 05 — Communication Style

### Deep Questions

- On a scale of 1-10, how direct are you? Do you soften criticism or deliver it straight?
- How formal is your default register? How does it shift by context?
- How do you deliver bad news?
- How do you give feedback — direct and specific, or wrapped in encouragement?
- How do you adjust your communication for different audiences (technical vs non-technical, peers vs clients)?
- Are you warm, neutral, or reserved in professional communication?
- How do you handle disagreement in public versus private?

### Good vs Mediocre

**Mediocre:** "I adapt my communication to the audience."

**Good:** "Default register is direct and professional — 7/10 on formality. I drop to 5/10 on social media and rise to 8/10 for client-facing documents. I deliver criticism directly but with specifics, not generalities: 'The third paragraph contradicts the opening thesis' rather than 'This needs work.' I open with agreement where genuine before diverging: 'You're right about X, but Y is where I see it differently.' Bad news gets delivered early in the message, not buried at the end."

### Common Pitfalls

- Claiming to "adapt to the audience" without specifying how.
- Not addressing how you handle conflict or disagreement.
- Describing only one register. Most people shift by context — capture the range.

## 06 — Decision-Making

### Deep Questions

- How quickly do you make decisions? Are you a rapid decider or a deliberate researcher?
- What is your risk tolerance? Do you move fast and accept failures, or minimise risk at the cost of speed?
- What data or evidence do you require before committing to a course of action?
- Do you use any explicit frameworks (first principles, pros/cons, decision matrices)?
- How do you handle decisions with incomplete information?
- What is your approach to reversible versus irreversible decisions?
- When do you seek input from others versus deciding alone?

### Good vs Mediocre

**Mediocre:** "I try to make informed decisions based on data."

**Good:** "Reversible decisions get made fast — within a day, often within hours. Irreversible decisions (pricing models, technology stack choices, public commitments) get at least three days of research and a written analysis. I am comfortable with 70% confidence for most decisions and do not chase 95% certainty. I use a simple framework: What is the worst case? Can I recover from it? If yes, move. I rarely seek consensus but often seek input — I want diverse perspectives, not permission."

### Common Pitfalls

- Being generic about data-driven decision-making without specifics.
- Not distinguishing between types of decisions (high-stakes versus routine).
- Omitting your relationship with uncertainty and incomplete information.

## 07 — Working Style

### Deep Questions

- What does a typical working day look like for you?
- What tools do you use daily? What is your primary development environment?
- How do you manage tasks and priorities?
- Do you work in focused blocks or context-switch frequently?
- How do you prefer to collaborate — asynchronously or synchronously?
- What are your non-negotiable work habits?
- How do you handle interruptions?
- What is your relationship with deadlines?

### Good vs Mediocre

**Mediocre:** "I use modern tools and work efficiently."

**Good:** "I work in 90-minute deep blocks, typically three per day. Primary tools: VS Code with Claude Code, terminal-based workflows, Git for everything. I manage tasks in a combination of Notion and markdown files — not project management software. All collaboration is asynchronous by default. I check email twice daily, not continuously. I ship small increments frequently rather than large releases."

### Common Pitfalls

- Listing tools without describing how you use them.
- Describing an idealised workflow rather than your actual one.
- Ignoring collaboration preferences and boundaries.

## 08 — Knowledge & Expertise

### Deep Questions

- What are the three topics you could speak about for an hour without preparation?
- What areas do you have strong opinions on but limited deep expertise?
- Where are your genuine blind spots? What topics should you not be generating authoritative content about?
- What credentials, qualifications, or experience back up your expertise claims?
- What is the difference between what you know and what your audience thinks you know?
- Are there emerging areas you are actively learning about?

### Good vs Mediocre

**Mediocre:** "I have deep expertise in software development and AI."

**Good:** "Deep expertise: Full-stack JavaScript/TypeScript development, AI-assisted development workflows, solo SaaS product development, educational technology. Strong opinions but developing depth: AI trading strategies, programmatic content creation. Aware but not expert: machine learning model training, mobile native development (I use cross-platform frameworks). Blind spots: hardware, networking infrastructure, enterprise-scale systems. I never generate authoritative content about areas outside my deep expertise without explicit flagging."

### Common Pitfalls

- Claiming expertise too broadly. Everyone is a non-expert in most things.
- Not identifying blind spots. These are critical for preventing AI from generating content that overreaches.
- Listing skills rather than describing depth levels.

## 09 — Aspirations

### Deep Questions

- Where do you want to be professionally in one year? In five years?
- What does success look like for your current project?
- What would you build if resources were unlimited?
- What legacy do you want to leave in your field?
- What skills are you actively developing?
- What opportunities are you watching for?

### Good vs Mediocre

**Mediocre:** "I want to grow my business and make an impact."

**Good:** "12-month goal: Ship two profitable SaaS products generating recurring revenue, building towards a sustainable solo software business. 3-year vision: A portfolio of AI-augmented tools that each serve a specific professional need, with enough recurring revenue to be my primary income. I am building towards the model of the AI-augmented solo developer — one person with AI tools competing at the level of a small team. Success means profitable products, not growth metrics."

### Common Pitfalls

- Being too vague. "Make an impact" means nothing without specifics.
- Confusing aspirations with fantasies. Include only goals you are actively working towards.
- Not distinguishing timescales.

## 10 — Relationships

### Deep Questions

- Who is your primary audience? Describe them specifically.
- What does your audience already know versus what do they need to learn?
- Who are your key collaborators or partners?
- Who are your competitors? How do you differentiate from them?
- What communities are you part of? What is your role in them?
- Who do you serve that you do not charge? (Open source, community, mentoring)

### Good vs Mediocre

**Mediocre:** "My audience is tech professionals interested in AI."

**Good:** "Primary audience: Solo developers and small teams (1-5 people) building software products, typically technical founders. They know how to code but are exploring AI-assisted workflows. Secondary audience: Educators using technology, specifically UK primary and secondary teachers. Competitors in the productivity tool space: generic AI writing tools (Jasper, Copy.ai) — I differentiate by building specific tools rather than general-purpose ones. Community: active in the Claude Code user community and indie hacker spaces."

### Common Pitfalls

- Describing the audience you wish you had rather than the one you have.
- Ignoring competitors. Understanding your competitive landscape sharpens your positioning.
- Not distinguishing primary from secondary audiences.

## 11 — Content & Creation

### Deep Questions

- What formats do you create in? (Blog posts, threads, videos, documentation, courses)
- What platforms do you publish on? What works best where?
- What is your quality bar? When is something ready to publish?
- How often do you publish? Is this sustainable?
- What is your content creation process from idea to publication?
- What role does AI play in your content creation?
- What types of content do you refuse to create?

### Good vs Mediocre

**Mediocre:** "I create content across multiple platforms to build my brand."

**Good:** "Formats: Technical blog posts (1,000-2,000 words), X threads (5-10 tweets), LinkedIn posts (under 300 words), product documentation. Publishing cadence: 2-3 LinkedIn posts per week, one thread per week, one blog post per month. Quality bar: Every claim must be substantiated. No filler paragraphs. If I cannot say something specific, I do not publish. AI role: First-draft generation and editing assistance. All content is human-reviewed and often substantially rewritten. I always disclose AI assistance when asked."

### Common Pitfalls

- Describing intent rather than practice. What do you actually publish, not what do you plan to?
- Not specifying the quality bar. "High quality" is not a standard.
- Omitting the role of AI in your content process.

## 12 — Brand & Visual

### Deep Questions

- Do you have defined brand colours, fonts, or visual guidelines?
- Is your personal voice identical to your brand voice, or do they differ?
- What visual style represents your brand? (Minimalist, bold, technical, warm)
- How do you handle brand consistency across platforms?
- Do you use a logo? What does it communicate?

### Good vs Mediocre

**Mediocre:** "My brand is modern and professional."

**Good:** "Brand colours: dark navy (#1a1a2e), white, and an accent teal (#16c79a). Typography: Inter for headings, system fonts for body. Visual style: clean, minimal, technical — no decorative elements. Personal voice and brand voice are nearly identical since it is a one-person company, but the brand voice is slightly more formal and uses 'we' sparingly when referring to company decisions. Logo: geometric mark, no mascot."

### Common Pitfalls

- Skipping this dimension if you think you do not have a brand. You do — even if it is informal.
- Not addressing the personal/brand voice distinction.
- Being vague about visual identity when specifics exist.

## 13 — Emotional Texture

### Deep Questions

- What topics make you genuinely enthusiastic? How does that enthusiasm show in your writing?
- What frustrates you in your industry? How do you express that frustration?
- How do you handle vulnerability in professional content?
- What is your comfort level with humour? What kind of humour do you use?
- How do you express empathy or support?
- What emotions are off-limits in your professional communication?

### Good vs Mediocre

**Mediocre:** "I'm passionate about technology and sometimes get frustrated with the industry."

**Good:** "Genuine enthusiasm surfaces when discussing AI-assisted development workflows — the writing becomes more specific, examples multiply, and sentences get slightly longer. Frustration shows up as dry observations rather than complaints: 'This tool has been in beta for three years, which tells you everything you need to know.' I use humour sparingly and dryly — no jokes, no exclamation marks, but occasional understatement for effect. I do not express anger, anxiety, or personal vulnerability in professional content. Empathy shows through specificity: acknowledging the exact challenge someone faces rather than generic encouragement."

### Common Pitfalls

- Ignoring this dimension because it feels "soft." Emotional texture is what separates human writing from AI-generated content.
- Not specifying what emotions are off-limits. This is as important as what you express.
- Describing emotions rather than how they manifest in your communication.

---

## 14 — Examples & Samples

### Deep Questions

- Do you have 3-5 pieces of writing that are unmistakably "you"?
- What makes each piece representative? Can you annotate the specific elements?
- Do you have examples across different formats and contexts?
- Can you include an example of what your writing should NOT look like?

### Good vs Mediocre

**Mediocre:** Dropping in raw text with no context or annotation.

**Good:** Each sample annotated with:
- Why it was selected
- What dimensions it demonstrates
- Specific elements to notice (openings, rhythm, vocabulary choices, emotional texture)
- The context it was written in (platform, audience, purpose)

### Common Pitfalls

- Including too few samples. Aim for 5-10 across different contexts.
- Not annotating. Raw samples without explanation leave too much to interpretation.
- Only including your best work. Include typical work — that is what the AI needs to match.

## 15 — Evolution Log

### Deep Questions

- What has changed about your persona in the last six months?
- What positions have you softened or hardened on?
- What new dimensions have you added or significantly revised?
- Are there things you used to say or do that you have stopped?

### Good vs Mediocre

**Mediocre:** Leaving this dimension empty until "something changes."

**Good:**
```
## 2026

### March
- Updated 02 (Philosophy): Strengthened position on AI-assisted development after
  six months of production use. Moved from "cautiously optimistic" to "this is the
  default now."
- Added to 04 (Anti-Patterns): Never claim AI replaces developers. It augments.
- Retired from 11 (Content & Creation): Stopped publishing on Medium. All long-form
  now goes to personal blog.
```

### Common Pitfalls

- Not starting this dimension immediately. Begin logging from day one.
- Only recording large changes. Small shifts accumulate into significant evolution.
- Not dating entries. The timeline matters.
